Abstract

Generalizable 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) based on feed-forward inference has become a prominent direction in scene reconstruction. Existing methods mainly follow two paradigms: the first trains specialized networks from scratch; constrained by the scale of 3D datasets, these models generalize poorly to unseen scenes. The second introduces frozen foundation models as visual representation extractors; while this improves generalization, frozen parameters make the extracted features suboptimal for complex 3D geometry, and reliance on deep features induces spectral bias, causing high-frequency geometric details to be over-smoothed. To address these problems, we propose AdaptSplat, which adapts the visual foundation model at both the architecture level and the feature level. At the architecture level, DINOv3-ConvNeXt is adopted as the backbone: its convolutional architecture is memory-friendly and supports end-to-end training on standard hardware, while its native multi-scale features provide a natural basis for high-frequency prior extraction. The backbone is embedded as a differentiable component within the feed-forward 3DGS optimization loop, so that gradients from the rendering loss back-propagate directly through it, enabling active adaptation to multi-view geometric constraints. At the feature level, a lightweight Frequency-Preserving Adapter (FPA) is introduced, which uses 2D Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) to extract direction-aware high-frequency structural priors from shallow features, and injects them into the reconstruction network via a dual mechanism: high-frequency positional encodings are injected into the Q-K attention space of the expert network, and spatially adaptive residual modulation in the decoder resists secondary high-frequency attenuation from interpolation. Extensive experiments show that AdaptSplat achieves state-of-the-art feed-forward reconstruction performance on multiple standard benchmarks, with stable generalization across domains.

Insight

Comparison of existing paradigms and AdaptSplat. (a) Specialized networks trained from scratch suffer from weak generalization due to limited 3D data scale. (b) Frozen foundation models improve generalization but induce spectral bias, over-smoothing high-frequency details. (c) AdaptSplat adapts at both the architecture and feature levels, achieving strong generalization and spectral-unbiasedness simultaneously.

Qualitative Results

RE10K visual comparison. AdaptSplat produces sharper boundaries and finer texture details compared to Long-LRM and MVP.

DL3DV qualitative comparison. AdaptSplat yields superior high-frequency fidelity and sharper geometric boundaries. On complex scenes such as overlapping glassware or high-frequency tabletop textures, Long-LRM and MVP exhibit noticeable blurring, while AdaptSplat closely matches the ground truth.
